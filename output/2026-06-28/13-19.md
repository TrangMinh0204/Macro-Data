They all faced the same grim reality: Nearly three days had elapsed since the 7.2- and 7.5-magnitude quakes, and the search for survivors trapped under the rubble had turned desperate across much of the region.
Many of the calls made by rescue workers, searching for buried survivors, are met by silence in the ruins of cities and towns across the state, La Guaira. Many feared that time was running out. Aid groups say that the first 72 hours after a disaster are the most critical for finding survivors.
“We don’t really know how many people are under the rubble,” the top U.N. official based in Venezuela, Gianluca Rampolla, said in an interview.
“The estimation we have at this point is that the number of collapsed buildings is 125,” he said, adding that such a figure would suggest many more deaths than the latest official figure.
The disaster has increased pressure on Venezuela’s leader, Ms. Rodríguez, and on President Trump, who helped bring her to power. After U.S. forces seized the country’s longtime dictator, Nicolás Maduro, Mr. Trump embraced her leadership while also saying he expected the United States could potentially “run” and “rebuild” Venezuela for years.
The United States intends to take a leading role in the response, a senior State Department official said on Saturday. Nearly 250 Americans have joined the efforts so far, he said, including teams from Virginia, California and Florida, and the United States has deployed a naval ship, the U.S.S. Fort Lauderdale, to provide medical support.
